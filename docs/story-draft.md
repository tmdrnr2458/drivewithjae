## English Story Options

### Option A (Recommended)

I did my two years in the Korean military — learned how to get up when things knock you down. After that, I came to America by myself. No family here, no safety net. I got scammed. I made bad decisions because I didn't know better and had nobody to ask. But all of that taught me one thing: when you're far from home and don't have the right information, the only thing that matters is whether you can trust the person sitting across from you. Now I work at a dealership, and I see people every day who are in the same spot I was — navigating something complicated in a language that isn't theirs. I know what that feels like, and I don't want anyone to go through what I went through.

### Option B

I served two years in the Korean military, then moved to America alone. No connections, no guide. I got taken advantage of more than once — the kind of mistakes you make when you don't speak the language and don't know who to trust. That experience is why I do what I do now. At the dealership, I meet people every week who remind me of myself back then — trying to figure out a big purchase in a country that's still unfamiliar. I know how it feels to sit across from someone and wonder if they're being straight with you.

## Korean Story Options

군대 2년 하고, 혼자 미국에 왔어요. 아는 사람도 없고, 물어볼 데도 없고. 그러다 보니까 사기도 당하고, 몰라서 손해 본 적도 여러 번이에요. 그때 제일 크게 느낀 게, 결국 사람이에요. 내 편이 되어줄 사람이 있느냐 없느냐가 전부더라고요. 지금 딜러십에서 일하면서 보면, 영어가 어렵거나 정보가 없어서 불리한 분들이 진짜 많아요. 저도 그랬으니까요. 그래서 저는 이 일을 할 때, 제가 당했던 그 막막한 기분을 절대 느끼게 하고 싶지 않아요.

## Recommendations

### Title
- **EN: "How I Got Here"** (Option B) — more conversational, less poetic/corporate. Matches the tone of the rest of the site.
- **KO: "여기 오기까지"** (Option A, keep current) — already perfect. Natural Korean, no need to change.

### Story
- **EN: Option A** — Slightly longer but covers every beat (military, alone, scammed, trust, now helping). The last line ("I don't want anyone to go through what I went through") shows empathy without promising anything. It lets the reader draw their own conclusion about trustworthiness.
- **KO: Option A** — The rhythm feels like someone actually talking. The ending ("제가 당했던 그 막막한 기분을 절대 느끼게 하고 싶지 않아요") is the emotional anchor — it doesn't say "trust me," it says "I remember that helpless feeling and I refuse to let you feel it." Korean readers will connect with this because it's specific and personal, not a slogan.

### Why these work for the Korean audience
- No 존댓말 overload (해요체 throughout, not 하십시오체)
- No corporate promises ("최고의 서비스" etc.)
- The scam reference is matter-of-fact ("사기도 당하고, 몰라서 손해 본 적도 여러 번이에요") — not dramatic, just honest
- Military is mentioned in one breath, not glorified
- The ending earns trust by showing vulnerability, not by claiming virtue
